Boardroom Tea: The last stand for Philippine education

Education | February 5, 2026 10:39am | by SALVE IBAŇEZ

Two days ago, the Executive Director of the Second Congressional Commission on Education Dr. Karol Mark Yee delivered an intervention letter wrapped in policy prose.

"The Philippine education system is at a decisive moment, defined not only by the sobering evidence of a learning crisis, but also by an unprecedented convergence of alignment, momentum, and collective action."

EDCOM 2's Final Report entitled "Fixing the Foundations" reads like the clearest, most honest diagnosis of Philippine education—revealed while the patient is barely alive but still breathing. The report weaponized three years of research into a National Education and Workforce Development Plan that functions as a 10-year educational roadmap for the education sector.

In the report, Dr. Yee and his team methodically documented the Philippines' descent into academic neglect: Most Grade 3 students were 1-2 years behind curriculum expectations during foundational learning years. By Grade 6, only 19.56 percent achieve proficiency. The carnage accelerates—just 0.74 percent and 0.4 percent reach proficiency in Grades 10 and 12 respectively.

That's not educational progression—that's systematic academic deterioration. But who is this guy who dares to speak the hard truths?

Yee brings formidable intellectual artillery to this crisis: a Cambridge PhD, Harvard master's degree (as a Fulbright Scholar), and the rare combination of analytical precision with policy implementation experience. His previous stint as CHED Executive Director taught him that brilliant reports mean nothing without execution muscle.

Heading the think tank for something as critical as our education system also means he has to answer to a lot of bosses. The commission has, as co-chairpersons, Senator Bam Aquino, Senator Loren Legarda, Rep. Roman Romulo, and Rep. Jude Acidre. Its commissioners include Sen. Sherwin Gatchalian, Sen. Joel Villanueva, Rep. Steve Solon, Rep. Zia Alonto Adiong, and Rep. Anna Victoria Veloso-Tuazon.

Then you have the different Cabinet Secretaries who have a stake in the recovery of the education sector.

The Angara Wild Card

Speaking of Cabinet secretaries, I have missed the witty and very tito Instagram stories of Education Secretary Sonny Angara. While denying involvement in flood control project anomalies, the controversy engulfs his Sigma Rho fraternity brother Trygve Olaivar, who resigned as DepEd undersecretary after being linked to alleged budget insertion schemes. I know he continues to navigate treacherous political waters, although the noise has died down a bit.

These distractions arrive precisely when focus is crucial. A historic P1.37 trillion education budget has been proposed for FY 2026, with P961 billion for DepEd alone—the largest allocation to any national government agency. Yet education spending remains at 3.6 percent of GDP, below UNESCO's 4-6 percent benchmark and trailing regional competitors.

These are problems that Sec. Angara has merely inherited. The governance architecture amplifies dysfunction: DepEd participates in at least 261 institutional bodies, twenty-one jointly attended with TESDA and CHED. Performance targets cascade inconsistently—the Philippine Development Plan sets broad goals, the General Appropriations Act attaches budgets to different indicators, while individual commitments often track compliance outputs rather than learning outcomes.
